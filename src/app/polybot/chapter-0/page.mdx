# Chapter 0 — Why prediction markets?

A prediction market is a place where people bet on whether something will
happen. *Will the Fed cut rates in March? Will the Lakers make the playoffs?
Will GDP exceed 3% this quarter?* Each question is a contract. You can buy YES
or NO, usually for somewhere between one cent and ninety-nine cents. If the
event resolves in your favor, your contract pays out a dollar. If it
doesn&apos;t, it pays zero.

This sounds like sports betting, and structurally it is. But two things make
prediction markets interesting from a trading perspective:

1. **Prices look like probabilities.** A YES contract trading at 72 cents is
   the market&apos;s collective guess that the event will happen 72% of the
   time. That makes them mathematically tractable in a way that, say, equity
   prices are not.

2. **They are not perfectly efficient.** Stock markets have decades of
   academic attention, professional market makers, and high-frequency arb
   firms shaving milliseconds. Prediction markets have... a few crypto
   degens, some retail bettors, and a small number of quant shops. The slop
   is much bigger.

That second point is what this curriculum is about. The slop is real, it&apos;s
measurable, and you can capture some of it with code that fits in a single
repo.

## Three kinds of slop

Over the next chapters we&apos;ll exploit three specific kinds of inefficiency.
This is not a survey — these are the three I built strategies for in my own
bot, the three where I have actual data showing they exist on real exchanges
right now.

### 1. Logic arbitrage

Prediction markets often list *threshold families.* For example, on Kalshi:

- Will GDP exceed 1%?
- Will GDP exceed 2%?
- Will GDP exceed 3%?

These three are mathematically related: anyone who wins the third one also
wins the first two. So the price of "GDP exceeds 3%" must be less than or
equal to the price of "GDP exceeds 2%". When it isn&apos;t — when the prices
go non-monotonic by even a cent — there&apos;s a guaranteed-profit trade. You
buy the cheap one, sell the expensive one, and collect the difference no
matter what GDP actually does.

These mispricings show up. Not constantly, but frequently enough that a
scanner running every fifteen minutes will catch a few per day across the
hundreds of threshold families on a typical exchange. We&apos;ll build that
scanner in chapter 3.

### 2. Short arbitrage

In multi-outcome markets — say, "who wins the 2026 Super Bowl?" — exactly one
outcome will resolve YES and all the others will resolve NO. So the YES
prices across all outcomes should sum to roughly 100 cents. (Roughly, not
exactly — fees and the bid-ask spread create a small wedge.)

When the YES sum exceeds 100 cents by more than the wedge, you can buy NO on
every outcome. One of them will lose, but the rest will all pay out a dollar
each. The math guarantees a profit if you can fill all the legs.

The catch is the "if you can fill all the legs." We&apos;ll spend a whole
chapter on the orderbook depth checks that decide whether a strategy actually
trades or just generates pretty alerts.

### 3. The favorite-longshot bias

This one is the most interesting and the most fragile. Across many years of
sports betting and prediction markets, researchers have observed that
**favorites are systematically underpriced and longshots are systematically
overpriced.** A contract trading at 72 cents wins, on average, more than 72%
of the time. A contract trading at 5 cents wins less than 5% of the time.

The size of the bias depends on the platform, the category, and the time
period. Most academic papers report it as a gentle curve. When I calibrated
my own bias model on 17,258 settled Kalshi markets, I found something
sharper: the edge is concentrated in the 70-to-85 cent range on economics
markets specifically. A contract trading at 75 cents on Kalshi&apos;s
economics category wins about 87% of the time — twelve points above its
listed price.

Twelve points sounds huge. It is. It is also fragile. In sports markets it
disappears. In culture and politics markets it&apos;s weak. On Polymarket it
behaves differently. Half of building a working FLB strategy is figuring out
where the edge actually lives and refusing to trade everywhere else.

## What we&apos;ll build

By the end of this curriculum you will have:

- A working Python codebase that fetches live market data from Polymarket and
  Kalshi.
- Three scanners — one for each kind of inefficiency — that produce
  structured opportunity records as JSON.
- A validator that runs eight checks against each opportunity before any
  capital is committed.
- A position sizer that uses the Kelly criterion (and explains why
  half-Kelly is what serious people actually use).
- A paper-trading harness that simulates fills realistically enough to
  actually mean something.
- A live-trading mode with a three-layer safety system that makes it
  structurally hard to deploy real money by accident.
- A small dashboard that shows you what the bot is doing and why.

That&apos;s a lot. We&apos;ll build it incrementally, one chapter at a time,
and every chapter ends with a runnable script that does something real.

## The honest part

Most strategies don&apos;t work. In my own bot, I tested five FLB variants —
Kalshi economics, Kalshi sports, Kalshi culture/politics, Kalshi
weather/other, and Polymarket sports. **One of them is profitable.** The
other four were killed because the win rate didn&apos;t clear the breakeven
threshold after fees, even on data that looked promising at first.

The point of this curriculum is not to hand you a working money-printer.
The point is to give you the infrastructure — the scanners, the validators,
the sizers, the safety systems — so that when *you* find an edge, you have
the tools to capture it carefully. The edges are out there. Most of them are
small, fragile, and conditional. Capturing them takes engineering as much as
it takes finding them.

If you&apos;re ready, [Chapter 1](/polybot/chapter-1) is your first
runnable Python script. You&apos;ll see real prediction-market prices in
your terminal in under five minutes, no signup required.
